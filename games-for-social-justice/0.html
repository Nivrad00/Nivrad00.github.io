Why care about video games?

  • Games are popular – 67% of Americans play them
  • Games can be deeply transformative
  • Games can introduce players to experiences other than their own

Video games are often written off as mindless fun. But like any other form of art – books, movies, or music – games can have purposes other than entertainment. These so-called serious games have been applied to countless fields, from language learning to physical therapy. But can they play a part in social justice?

In short, games put players in other people's shoes. Through non-normative mechanics, narratives, and characters, games can provide a voice for those on the margins, and foster awareness and empathy in others. Other games have personal uses, such as training for those with disabilities or escapism for those with marginalized gender identities. As a form of media with a broad appeal and a potential for deep immersion, video games are perfectly suited to nurture a more compassionate world.

What's in this guide?

This brief annotated bibliography is intended to demonstrate the breadth and power of video games that further social justice, with a focus on American audiences. It is organized by social issue, with each section containing:

  • Recommended games that address social justice
  • Popular and academic articles discussing the impact of such games

Who's it for?

  • New researchers surveying the field of serious games
  • Librarians and educators curating games or using them in lessons and programming
  • Individuals looking for game recommendations

What's not covered?

The guide focuses on games with social justice as a primary purpose. Many important issues have been excluded due to scope constraints, including:

  • Sexist or harmful portrayals of characters in video games
  • Violent games and the military industrial complex
  • Harrassment and hate in game communities
  • Game accessibility
  • Video games and the environment
  • Game design and development
  • Toxicity in the game development industry
